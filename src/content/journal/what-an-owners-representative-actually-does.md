Most people meet construction for the first time in the middle of their own
project — and by then, the structure of the relationship is already set. The
general contractor holds the schedule, the budget, the subcontractors, and the
information. You hold the checkbook and a lot of questions.

An owner's representative changes that arrangement. The role is exactly what it
sounds like: a construction professional who sits on **your** side of the table,
whose only obligation is to your interests.

## The core idea: one point of accountability

On a typical project, accountability is distributed — which is another way of
saying it belongs to no one. The architect points to the contractor, the
contractor points to a sub, the sub points to a change order, and you are left
trying to referee a game whose rules you were never taught.

An owner's representative consolidates that accountability into a single person
who answers to you. We read the contracts before you sign them. We review the
bids line by line. We walk the site, ask the uncomfortable questions, and tell
you what we find — plainly.

## What that looks like week to week

- **Reviewing pay applications** against work that has actually been completed,
  not work that has merely been promised.
- **Pressure-testing change orders** so a $1,200 revision doesn't quietly become
  a $12,000 one.
- **Holding the schedule** and flagging slippage early, while it is still
  cheap to fix.
- **Translating** — turning the language of construction into decisions you can
  actually make.

## Why it pays for itself

Good representation tends to recover its own cost several times over: in bids
that are scoped correctly the first time, in change orders that don't survive
scrutiny, and in a schedule that doesn't drift into another season. But the
quieter return is the one clients mention most — they got to enjoy their project
instead of managing it.

That is the entire point. You hired professionals to build something. You should
get to think about the result, not the referee.
